My Journey to Medicine

I am Mexican American and Native American. I think my most important characteristic as a doctor is the fact that I listen, when so many others don't have the time or worse, don't take the time. I take tremendous pride in what I do, and this is my story.

Believe it or not, my undergraduate degree was in music education, and my first career was as an elementary school music teacher. I went to music school in New Mexico, where my parents are from, and later taught on the Blackfeet Reservation in northern Montana, then ended up in the DC metropolitan area until I moved to Richmond, Virginia, to attend the Medical College of Virginia.

From Music Teacher to Family Doctor

How did I get from music teacher to family doctor? It was sudden when my mother called me on Halloween 1993 and asked me why I wasn't in medical school. Within a week, I was enrolled for the next semester taking prerequisite coursework. Operating at the same breakneck speed, I took the MCAT exams in 1994 and started medical school in 1995.

Basically, my mother's question was asked at the right time in my life, and it suddenly made perfect sense. I am a Sun Dancer, and I had never understood why I was called to be a dancer with what I was doing in my secular life. Then it dawned on me - the Native American Sun Dance is a healing ceremony. At that realization, becoming a doctor just fit that spiritual context and seemed like the natural culmination of my life. And I realized at that time that my career was going to be dedicated to providing medical care to underserved people.

My Professional Path

I ended up in Missouri by matching for residency training at Truman Lakewood, formerly called Truman East. I graduated in 2002 and became a board-certified Family Physician. I worked for 5 years at the Samuel Rogers Community Health Center in Lexington, Missouri, then opened my own practiceâ€”the first woman doctor in Grain Valley in 2007, and now in Blue Springs. I have basically spent my career being the "Family Doctor Near You." During this time, I also became a board-certified obesity medicine physician.

A Heritage of Service

My dad was in the Army, and that kept us moving when I was young, so really, I am from everywhere. I started public school in Germany and finished it in Alaska. My parents both came from humble Spanish families in northern New Mexico. My dad was the first member of his family to graduate from college. Their careers after dad got out of the Army have been dedicated to helping the underserved. Even after they retired, through work for their church, they continued to serve the immigrant communities. They celebrated their 70th wedding anniversary before my father passed on last year at the age of 92. I was greatly influenced by them. My desire to care for underserved patients was born in me. I was raised with it.

Who Can Benefit?

Pelvic floor disorders affect millions of women, including urinary incontinence, overactive bladder, and pelvic organ prolapse. Research shows that at least 1 in 3 women experience these conditions, with prevalence increasing with age, childbirth, and menopause.

Treatment with CoreLift has shown symptom improvement rates of up to 82% for urinary urgency incontinence, 92% for nocturia, and 87% for bladder emptying issues.

What to Expect

CoreLift with Emsella uses electromagnetic technology to stimulate pelvic floor muscles. You remain fully clothed and simply sit on the Emsella chair for 28 minutes. Most patients require 6 sessions over 3 weeks, with many noticing improvements after just 2-3 treatments.
